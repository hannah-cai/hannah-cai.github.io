Bickham Script: Typeface Spread

InDesign

—

Prompt:

Write a three paragraph statement that highlights the unique characteristics and personality of your typeface in the context of its use and in relation to its larger type family, if that exists. Using your written text, create a two-page spread that introduces the reader to the typeface you were assigned. Incorporate at least one image, but no more than three (make sure to include image credits). Clarity, communication and readability is your goal. Consider the use of running headers and/or footers and page folios.

Process

1—research + writing

Before I started to do the writing that would eventually go into the spread, I did a bit of research, and found that Bickham Script has a rich history that extends back to centuries ago. Essentially, the creator of the typeface, Richard Lipton, found a book called "The Universal Penman" by chance while in a library, and fell in love with the calligraphic script within it. That script was written in the 1800s by a master calligrapher named George Bickham; Lipton's typeface is his own tribute to the Bickham's intricate work. There are a lot more details; read the text in the spread for the whole story.

2—thumbnails

After the text was set, I started doing thumbnail sketches onto small printouts of a 5-column grid.

3—moodboarding

Gathered images and inspiration for possible graphics to use in my spread.

4—iteration

Many of these iterations were taken directly from my thumbnails. I worked to try and match my spread with the characteristics of my assigned typeface: flowing, formal, elegant, embellished, established, playful, and dramatic.

5—refinement

I narrowed my focus down to this layout, because I felt like it reflected my typeface's characteristics the most.

6—final

Here is my final deliverable; the main refinements I made to this version were:

1. change the color of the into/hook to make it easier to read

2. move the pink curlique so it matches the form of the text

3. cut down the text to add room for a few ligature examples, so that there's more Bickham Script showing
